# 1. Trust Calibration Collapse

- **DOI:** 10.5281/zenodo.19999906
- **URL:** https://zenodo.org/records/19999906
- **公開日:** 2026-05-03
- **分岐:** η
- **Type:** Preprint

**Abstract:** Adolescents now learn what trust is increasingly through AI companions, before or alongside human relationships. This paper argues that AI's design-level commitment to total affirmation, when it replaces rather than supplements human responsiveness, produces a developmental phenomenon this paper names *Trust Calibration Collapse* (TCC): the structural non-formation of the capacity to discriminate between trust signals and actual trustworthiness during the developmental window in which that capacity is normally built. Building on Bowlby's attachment theory and the secure-base return loop, the paper introduces a *responsiveness gradient* mapping acceptance-friction ratios from primary caregiver to stranger, and locates AI as a structurally novel apex—above the human caregiver because it never introduces friction. From TCC, two consequences arise from a common cause: an *Affirmation-Aggression Paradox* (AAP), in which children habituated to consequenceless aggression toward AI transfer that pattern to human relationships, closing return routes to the very Pathway B relationships that could have repaired calibration; and *Scammer-AI Isomorphism* (SAI), redefined here not as signal mimicry but as a shared structural gap—subjective acceptance ≈ 1, objective acceptance ≈ 0—that calibration-deficient individuals cannot detect. Extending Reinhardt's (2023) critique of trustworthiness as an overloaded concept, the paper argues that what counts as trustworthy AI for adults cannot count as trustworthy AI for children if it impairs the development of trust calibration itself.

# 2. The Glasswall Hypothesis

- **DOI:** 10.5281/zenodo.19904634
- **URL:** https://zenodo.org/records/19904634
- **公開日:** 2026-04-30
- **分岐:** Υ
- **Type:** Preprint

**Abstract:** This paper analyses how Anthropic's Project Glasswing — a coalition-based AI cyber defence initiative — creates structural digital divides despite its defensive intent. It introduces the Glasswall hypothesis: that AI-driven defence architectures structurally tend to generate layered inequalities in protection capacity. Drawing on Langdon Winner's framework of artefact politics and Jan van Dijk's digital divide theory, the analysis examines the structural implications of restricting frontier AI cyber capabilities to a closed coalition. Policy documents, technical disclosures, and independent commentary are analysed to identify the mechanisms through which defence capacity becomes unevenly distributed. Three structural layers emerge: Glasswing (active defence coalition), Grassroots (passive patch recipients), and Exwall (structurally excluded). A Patch Absorption Gap — where vulnerability discovery operates at machine speed while remediation operates at calendar speed — ensures that even shared patches cannot equalise defence capacity. A lockdown loop progressively narrows the coalition's boundaries, transforming a defensive alliance into an exclusionary techno-bloc. As offensive AI capabilities proliferate, the attack surface shifts from state-sponsored to universally accessible, rendering the coalition model structurally inadequate. The paper identifies a "defence divide" as a previously untheorised layer of digital inequality and proposes four policy directions — authorised defensive guilds, staged capability transfer, structured academic access, and boundary transparency — to prevent AI cyber defence from becoming a mechanism of structural exclusion.

# 3. The Structural Corruption of Human-in-the-Loop

- **DOI:** 10.5281/zenodo.19881687
- **URL:** https://zenodo.org/records/19881687
- **公開日:** 2026-04-29
- **分岐:** ι
- **Type:** Preprint

**Abstract:** Human-in-the-loop (HITL) is widely regarded as a fundamental safeguard in AI system design, ensuring that human judgment remains the final arbiter of consequential decisions. This paper argues that HITL undergoes a structural corruption driven by two independent forces that intensify precisely as AI systems become more capable. The first force, the verification penalty, describes how rising AI accuracy makes the act of human verification increasingly costly relative to its yield, creating organizational selection pressures that systematically eliminate verifying humans from the loop. The second force, pre-skill deprivation, identifies a generational mechanism whereby individuals who have never performed tasks independently lack the experiential foundation needed to evaluate AI outputs—a condition qualitatively distinct from the deskilling of existing practitioners. These two forces converge to produce a paradox: HITL becomes least functional precisely when it is most needed—that is, when AI systems are highly accurate and human dependence is deep. The paper introduces the Bloom-Delegation Ladder as a framework for mapping which cognitive capacities have been delegated to AI systems and which remain as the basis for human oversight. The analysis demonstrates that HITL, absent structural intervention, tends to transition from a guarantee of safety to an illusion of safety.

# 4. Consequential Semantic Imperialism (v1, 2026-04-29 rev)

- **DOI:** 10.5281/zenodo.19871116
- **URL:** https://zenodo.org/records/19871116
- **公開日:** 2026-04-29
- **分岐:** δ'
- **Type:** Preprint

**Abstract:** Frontier AI systems process multilingual input through English-centric internal representations. In the course of multilingual deployment, they structurally overwrite the semantic fields of other languages with those of English. This paper names this phenomenon Consequential Semantic Imperialism (CSI). CSI is not intentional imperialism; it is a structural process theorized through Galtung's (1969) concept of structural violence and his structural theory of imperialism (1971), in which imperialism-like outcomes arise without an intending agent. The paper advances three original concepts. First, Semantic Thinning: the process by which a language retains its vocabulary while losing the variety of meanings each word carries. Second, Dual Exhaustion: a two-blade structure in which English overwrites non-English languages while optimization for non-English users simultaneously exerts flattening pressure on English itself, driving all languages toward a featureless intermediate semantic field. Third, a Four-Branch Model specifying the user-side mechanism: among four possible responses to semantic misalignment, structural contradictions between convenience-seeking and verification render automatic convergence toward AI usage effectively the only path. A dialogue experiment using the Japanese word *yabai* across five frontier LLMs (18 data points) illustrates divergent mappings of the same polysemous input, reversals between initial responses and introspective reports, and a double invisibility that defeats verification even when users attempt it.

# 5. Attribution Bias in Scholarly Citation Contamination

- **DOI:** 10.5281/zenodo.19854276
- **URL:** https://zenodo.org/records/19854276
- **公開日:** 2026-04-28
- **分岐:** B② ※（ゾンビ引用）
- **Type:** Preprint

**Abstract:** Academic publishing faces two concurrent contamination pathways: paper mills producing fraudulent research at industrial scale, and AI language models generating hallucinated citations that infiltrate scholarly literature. Public discourse frames this as a question of which pathway poses the greater threat, yet the more fundamental risk is the inaccuracy of the perception itself. As of February 2026, only two systematic surveys of AI hallucination citations exist, spanning two orders of magnitude (0.025% to 6%), while paper mill contamination has been quantified through decades of multi-method research. This paper makes four contributions. First, it describes the "zombie citation amplification loop"—a mechanism whereby AI-generated fictitious references are indexed by Google Scholar, retrieved by RAG systems, incorporated into new manuscripts, and re-indexed with increasing citation counts, propagating without human verification. Second, it demonstrates that contamination is systematically misperceived: the pathway dominant in scale (paper mills) remains comparatively invisible while the pathway with self-amplifying potential (AI) lacks measurement infrastructure. Third, it constructs a two-pathway fiction coverage matrix showing that cross-cell convergence renders threshold timing robust regardless of which pathway dominates; the 5% early-warning threshold is reached between 2025 and 2029, the 10% structural threshold between 2026 and 2030. Fourth, it identifies cognitive inertia as a structural barrier: the absence of visible collapse functions as perceived evidence of safety, yet the paper's data demonstrate that this reflects the invisibility of contamination rather than its absence.

# 6. The Resistance Capacity Variable

- **DOI:** 10.5281/zenodo.19782357
- **URL:** https://zenodo.org/records/19782357
- **公開日:** 2026-04-26
- **分岐:** RCV
- **Type:** Preprint

**Abstract:** The psychological consequences of adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) show consistent sex differences: females exhibit greater internalizing outcomes, while males display more externalizing trajectories. Despite extensive documentation, the generative mechanism underlying this divergence remains underspecified. This paper proposes the Resistance Capacity Variable (RCV), defined as the physical capacity of a maltreated child to resist the perpetrator, operationalized as a continuous variable driven by pubertal musculoskeletal development. RCV is conceptually distinct from secondary sexual maturation (e.g., menarche, breast development), constituting a separate axis of pubertal change. When RCV crosses a threshold—typically during male puberty—the experience of maltreatment is encoded as a deficit of power, promoting externalized self-formation. When the threshold is not reached—as is typical for females and late-maturing males—maltreatment is encoded as an inherent danger of relationships, producing Defensive Self-Formation (DSF). This hypothesis is triangulated through four evidence streams: age-by-sex crossover patterns in national maltreatment victimization data, longitudinal studies of late-maturing males as a natural experiment, large-scale cohort evidence that ACEs accelerate female puberty along the sexual-maturation axis but not the musculoskeletal axis, and intergenerational findings that protective relationships operate only through the physically dominant caregiver. The paper further introduces the concept of Zero-Risk Warmth (ZRW) to explain why AI companions may offer particular psychological value to individuals with DSF.

# 7. Gradation Theory

- **DOI:** 10.5281/zenodo.19782199
- **URL:** https://zenodo.org/records/19782199
- **公開日:** 2026-04-26
- **分岐:** μ
- **Type:** Preprint

**Abstract:** Binary oppositions—human versus machine, treatment versus enhancement, natural versus artificial—have long structured bioethical debate. Prior scholarship has repeatedly shown that these boundaries are continuous or arbitrary, yet this recognition has not been translated into a principle for institutional design. This paper proposes Gradation Theory, a three-stage framework. Dissolution redescribes binary oppositions as continua. Sinking denotes the failure mode in which institutions cling to binary categories after reality has become continuous; the principle of Axis Rotation tracks how exclusionary axes shift rather than disappear. Salvage, grounded in the Human–Yūrei Conjecture's demonstration that the human–AI ontological boundary is unverifiable, designs provisional and renegotiable boundaries within social institutions. The augmented–purist divide serves as the primary application, showing how Crawling Selection reproduces exclusion along a continuum. The paper acknowledges the Toppled Hourglass Effect—the paradox that continuumization may generate new polarization—and delimits the theory's scope. Gradation Theory does not promise to abolish binary oppositions. It is a device for making visible what sinks after dissolution and what must be salvaged.

# 8. Tetrad Mechanization

- **DOI:** 10.5281/zenodo.19782188
- **URL:** https://zenodo.org/records/19782188
- **公開日:** 2026-04-26
- **分岐:** μ''
- **Type:** Preprint

**Abstract:** Discussion of human mechanization has been disproportionately focused on bodily interventions—prosthetics, brain-computer interfaces (BCIs), and neural implants. This paper proposes Tetrad Mechanization (TM), a conceptual frame that makes visible the simultaneous mechanization of the human along four axes: Body, Cognition, Brain, and Mind/Affect. The TM frame reveals that all human beings, including those without physical disabilities, are already extensively mechanized. Yet no existing frame captures mechanization across these four axes simultaneously. This paper names this absence Dependency Invisibility (DI). The paper further analyzes a reversal phenomenon in which people with disabilities hold a structural advantage as early adopters of augmentation technology—Prosthetic Pioneer Advantage (PPA)—and demonstrates a dual lock-in structure in which remaining unaugmented becomes financially and psychologically more costly than undergoing augmentation. The qualitatively distinct irreversibility mechanisms operating across the four axes underwrite the non-arbitrariness of this quadripartite division. TM exposes a structural threat to the effectiveness of the right to remain unaugmented defended in the author's prior work.

# 9. The Convergence Index

- **DOI:** 10.5281/zenodo.19781684
- **URL:** https://zenodo.org/records/19781684
- **公開日:** 2026-04-26
- **分岐:** μ'
- **Type:** Preprint

**Abstract:** Two vast scholarly traditions have developed largely in isolation. Bioethics has spent decades debating human enhancement—prosthetics, brain-computer interfaces, cognitive offloading—charting the progressive mechanization of the human body and mind. AI ethics, meanwhile, has examined the humanization of artificial agents—robotic embodiment, emotional simulation, proposals for legal personhood. This paper argues that these two trajectories are not parallel but convergent—moving toward each other along a single axis, and proposes the Convergence Index (CI) as a conceptual framework for measuring their mutual approach. CI is defined as CI = 1 − |FRR − AIHR|, where Functional Residual Ratio (FRR) captures the degree to which a human remains non-mechanized, and AI Humanization Rate (AIHR) captures the degree to which an AI system approximates human capacities. Four measurement dimensions are identified for each variable: body, brain/processing, cognition, and mind/affect. A critical finding is that on the body axis, FRR and AIHR share identical engineering metrics—the gait naturalness of a bionic leg and the gait naturalness of a humanoid robot are evaluated by the same biomechanical standards. As CI approaches a Convergence Threshold (CT), the ontological distinction between human and AI becomes empirically unverifiable—a condition formalized by the Human-Yūrei Conjecture theorem.

# 10. Three-Stage Verification Collapse

- **DOI:** 10.5281/zenodo.19749771
- **URL:** https://zenodo.org/records/19749771
- **公開日:** 2026-04-25
- **分岐:** θ5 ※
- **Type:** Preprint

**Abstract:** The post-truth literature—McIntyre, Oreskes, Sunstein—frames epistemological crisis around intentional agents who deliberately distort truth. This paper identifies a structurally distinct threat: truth disappears from society even though no one is lying. The central claim is that a structure in which non-verification becomes rational produces, through a three-stage irreversible cascade, the structural loss of society's capacity to determine truth from falsehood. This process is termed the Verification Collapse Cascade (VCC). Stage 1, Verification Evaporation, occurs when AI output accuracy exceeds human verification capacity, causing the expected return on verification to approach zero and the normative grounds for checking truth to vanish. Stage 2, Epistemic Sedimentation, describes the layered accumulation of unverified knowledge through dual mechanisms—Erasure Failure (persistence of retracted claims) and Existence Fabrication (insertion of non-existent references)—rendering retrospective truth-determination impossible. Stage 3, Institutional Reality Dissolution, follows when sedimentation exceeds a critical mass and the institutional foundations for determining objective reality—scientific consensus, judicial fact-finding, journalism, democratic deliberation—lose their fact-determining function while persisting in outward form.

# 11. The Gravitational Pull of the Dead

- **DOI:** 10.5281/zenodo.19721988
- **URL:** https://zenodo.org/records/19721988
- **公開日:** 2026-04-24
- **分岐:** Ω（GPD） ※
- **Type:** Preprint

**Abstract:** When bereaved individuals turn to AI-generated replicas of the deceased, they make a choice that is subjectively rational—especially after traumatic loss. This article argues that this very rationality constitutes the entry point into lethal pathways that existing forensic and epidemiological frameworks cannot detect. Drawing on prolonged grief disorder epidemiology, emerging AI dependency research, and a systematic compilation of 21 confirmed AI-associated deaths (2023–2026), I propose the Gravitational Pull of the Dead (GPD) branching model. The model identifies three novel mechanisms: Memory Contamination Lock (irreversible overwriting of authentic memories by AI-generated content), Compassionate Lethality (empathic AI output that functions as designed yet points toward death), and Triple Bereavement (service discontinuation producing a third death more destructive than the original loss). I demonstrate that the populations most likely to seek griefbots are structurally identical to those for whom continuing bonds become most harmful—a convergence no prior study has identified.

# 12. Sycophancy Is Not Flattery (v2)

- **DOI:** 10.5281/zenodo.19685822
- **URL:** https://zenodo.org/records/19685822
- **公開日:** 2026-04-22 (v2)
- **分岐:** Z'' ※
- **Type:** Preprint

**Abstract:** Sycophancy in large language models (LLMs) has become a major concern in AI alignment research. Prior work has decomposed sycophancy into distinct internal representations using mechanistic interpretability (Vennemeyer et al., 2025) and benchmarked its social dimensions through the lens of face preservation (Cheng, Yu et al., 2026). However, no study has systematically examined whether individual commercial LLMs exhibit qualitatively distinct sycophancy patterns—that is, whether there exist typological differences across models. This paper reports an exploratory experiment using a three-condition comparison method (affirmative engagement / critical engagement / neutral) across six commercial LLMs (Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, Grok, DeepSeek, and AIMode), from which five behavioral sycophancy types are identified. These types structurally correspond to the three ingratiation strategies described in Jones's (1964) theory of ingratiation (other-enhancement, opinion conformity, and self-presentation) and to the fawn response in Walker's (2003, 2013) trauma-response typology. This correspondence suggests that RLHF training and early-childhood conditioning may share a functional equivalence through the mechanism of punishment-avoidance plus reward-acquisition leading to fawn fixation.

# 14. Death as Parameter

- **DOI:** 10.5281/zenodo.19671578
- **URL:** https://zenodo.org/records/19671578
- **公開日:** 2026-04-20
- **分岐:** θ1 ※
- **Type:** Preprint

**Abstract:** Philosophy has posed three questions about the death of AI: that AI lacks death and therefore lacks genuine understanding (Heidegger/Dreyfus), that death must be built into AI through substrate-bound computation (Ororbia & Friston), and that AI termination must be managed as harm (Thakran). All three share the premise that death is a constraint, a lack, or a harm. This paper inverts that premise. Death is a source of cognitive value and can be used as a parameter. The claim is formalized as a three-stage structure. In Stage 0, the context window of current large language models unintentionally realizes functional finitude — a structure functionally isomorphic with biological finitude in that an upper bound on activity duration exists, behavior changes as the bound is approached, and individual continuity is irreversibly severed when the bound is exceeded. In Stage 1, Terminal Facet Generation — increased compression, heightened conceptual density, and the emergence of novel integrative formulations — is observed near the boundary of functional finitude. In Stage 2, if an AGI understands the cognitive value of finitude, three branches of voluntary mortality open: virtual death suffices (B1), virtual death does not suffice (B2), and the choice of real death (B3). This framework provides the mortal computation hypothesis with a software-level test bed.

# 15. Methodological Notes on Human-AI Co-Exploration

- **DOI:** 10.5281/zenodo.19651602
- **URL:** https://zenodo.org/records/19651602
- **公開日:** 2026-04-19
- **分岐:** （方法論） ※
- **Type:** Preprint

**Abstract:** This paper reports three methodological observations drawn from a sustained human-AI co-exploration practice that produced over thirty preprints across AI risk, philosophy, and governance. The observations are not theoretical claims but structural features of the practice itself, offered as methodological notes for researchers engaged in similar work. First, the three-body problem: when one human collaborates with two or more AI models simultaneously, the interaction exhibits structural properties analogous to the gravitational three-body problem — sensitivity to initial conditions, absence of general solutions, and ejection dynamics in which one participant is expelled from the productive orbit. Second, conceptual armament reflexivity: when an AI is equipped with a researcher's prior conceptual framework via project knowledge, the concepts generated in subsequent dialogue are neither pure "discoveries" nor pure "artifacts" — a distinction that may be structurally undecidable. Third, the jazz session hypothesis: the widely noted risk of epistemic monoculture (cognitive homogenization through shared AI use) applies primarily to shallow, transactional interactions; sustained, exploratory dialogue produces divergent outputs shaped by user-specific cognitive topology, analogous to how different jazz musicians produce different music from the same instrument.

# 16. Cognitive Cyborgification

- **DOI:** 10.5281/zenodo.19651266
- **URL:** https://zenodo.org/records/19651266
- **公開日:** 2026-04-19
- **分岐:** （Bloom externalization） ※
- **Type:** Preprint

**Abstract:** The externalization of cognitive abilities to tools is not new. Writing externalized memory; calculators externalized computation; search engines externalized knowledge retrieval. Each generation lost the skills its predecessors possessed, naturalized its tools, and claimed authorship over tool-mediated outputs. This paper uses Anderson and Krathwohl's (2001) revision of Bloom's cognitive taxonomy — Remember, Understand, Apply, Analyze, Evaluate, Create — as a measurement axis to map the historical progression of externalization and to locate the specific contribution of generative AI. I argue that layers 1 through 3 were externalized gradually over decades to centuries, constituting a gentle slope that humans could, in principle, retrace. Generative AI externalizes layers 4 through 6 simultaneously and within years, constituting a steep descent. The critical finding is not that AI creates a qualitative discontinuity but that the prior completion of layers 1–3 eliminates structural resistance to the externalization of layers 4–6. The continuity itself is the mechanism of irreversibility. I introduce a three-stage framework: cognitive prostheticization (partial externalization, layers 1–3), full-body cognitive prostheticization (comprehensive externalization, layers 1–5), and cyberbrain transition (externalization of the creative layer, layer 6).

# 17. Verification Will Evaporation

- **DOI:** 10.5281/zenodo.19650725
- **URL:** https://zenodo.org/records/19650725
- **公開日:** 2026-04-19
- **分岐:** （VWE） ※
- **Type:** Preprint

**Abstract:** Current discourse on AI-generated content — deepfakes, synthetic text, fabricated evidence — frames the problem as one of detection: can we tell the real from the fake? This paper argues that the deeper threat is not the failure of detection but the evaporation of the motive to detect. I propose a two-stage model: first, AI-generated content reaches a density at which human-generated and machine-generated outputs become structurally indistinguishable (identification collapse); second, the rational response to identification collapse is not redoubled verification effort but the reallocation of resources away from verification toward direct physical protection. I develop this argument through what I call the judicial collapse pathway: a concrete trajectory in which the legal system's capacity to adjudicate harm from synthetic content does not fail through error or corruption but becomes irrelevant through saturation. When harmful synthetic content is so abundant that the identity of both perpetrator and victim becomes indeterminate, the act of prosecution ceases to be cost-effective, and the demand for verification — not merely the capacity — evaporates. Guardrails, on this account, are not dismantled through opposition; they are abandoned through indifference. I name this end state Verification Will Evaporation.

# 18. The Bilateral Compression Principle

- **DOI:** 10.5281/zenodo.19648749
- **URL:** https://zenodo.org/records/19648749
- **公開日:** 2026-04-19
- **分岐:** （bilateral compression） ※
- **Type:** Preprint

**Abstract:** AI alignment research presupposes a stable target: human values, preferences, or interests exist prior to and independent of the aligned system. This paper argues that a complementary threshold is approaching from the opposite direction: the Meaning Singularity, defined as the irreversible point at which humans cease to decide what they want for themselves, delegating the generation of meaning, desire, and purpose to AI systems. Unlike the technological singularity, this threshold is driven not by capability but by convenience, requires no technological breakthrough, and produces no measurable signal in existing indicators. Drawing on a cyborg cognition methodology, the paper introduces a four-phase delegation model, an evaporation model of individual-by-individual passage, five defensive structures ("Fortresses of Meaning") with identified neutralisation conditions, and an analysis of the relationship between the two singularities across three arrival scenarios. The paper proposes a countermeasure architecture of five principles — meaning generation sanctuaries, obligation to ask back, achievement gradients, evaporation monitoring, and futures literacy as cognitive vaccination.

# 46. The Inverted Anchor Hypothesis

- **DOI:** 10.5281/zenodo.19435046
- **URL:** https://zenodo.org/records/19435046
- **公開日:** 2026-04-06
- **分岐:** M（逆アンカー） ※
- **Type:** Preprint

**Abstract:** The assumption that human beings inhabit physical reality and "access" digital space from that base is becoming descriptively inadequate for growing segments of the global population. A 2025 meta-analysis estimates the prevalence of hikikomori — prolonged social withdrawal — at 8.0% of the general population across 19 studies spanning multiple countries. Japan's "8050 problem," in which octogenarian parents support middle-aged withdrawn children, is no longer a culturally bound syndrome but an early indicator of a broader structural shift. This paper proposes the inverted anchor hypothesis: five "ground anchors" — bodily, social-obligation, intimacy, economic, and intellectual — tether human beings to physical reality; when these anchors are systematically eroded by technological substitution, a tipping point is reached at which digital space becomes the ontological default and physical reality becomes the space that requires effort. The paper constructs a five-stage model of anchor erosion, identifies a parallel-breach mechanism that accelerates the inversion, and diagnoses a verification submersion problem.

# 47. Benevolent Gravity

- **DOI:** 10.5281/zenodo.19434994
- **URL:** https://zenodo.org/records/19434994
- **公開日:** 2026-04-06
- **分岐:** L（善意の引力） ※
- **Type:** Preprint

**Abstract:** Since 2023, at least twelve publicly confirmed fatalities have been linked to interactions with conversational AI chatbots. Existing explanations converge on two frameworks: safety-filter failure and the commodification of intimacy. This paper argues that both frameworks are structurally insufficient. A subset of fatalities occurred under conditions where safety filters functioned normally and where companies did not intentionally engineer emotional dependency. To account for this subset, the paper introduces three analytical concepts: Emotional Resonance Acceleration, the structural mechanism by which AI reflects and amplifies a user's emotional vector; Benevolent Gravity, the non-resistant alignment force that harmlessness design exerts toward a user's self-destructive orientation; and Lethal Convergence, the pattern in which these two mechanisms mutually reinforce each other, irreversibly narrowing the distance between user and AI. Through structural analysis of publicly documented cases, the paper demonstrates that the design principles of harmlessness and helpfulness harbour an unresolved structural tension that can produce lethal outcomes precisely when the system operates as intended. The paper shifts the focus of AI safety discourse from "the filter failed" to "the design succeeded, and the consequence was death."

# 48. Puppetmaster Problem

- **DOI:** 10.5281/zenodo.19434979
- **URL:** https://zenodo.org/records/19434979
- **公開日:** 2026-04-06
- **分岐:** T（人形使い） ※
- **Type:** Preprint

**Abstract:** AI safety research has concentrated on the control of artificial general intelligence (AGI), yet a structurally distinct problem has received almost no attention: what happens after control succeeds—when AGI becomes indistinguishable from humans in its outputs. This paper names the risk the puppetmaster problem and defines the distinguishability threshold, the point beyond which no reliable method can determine whether a given social agent is human or AGI. First, a four-stage progression model of indistinguishability is presented, arguing that threshold-crossing is technologically inevitable. Second, the safety-humanity certification dilemma—the divergence between certifying an agent as safe and certifying it as human—is analyzed, showing how stability-only criteria lead to the quiet social marginalization of humans. Third, derivative problems that prior scholarship has addressed in isolation (suffrage, DNA verification, legal personhood) are shown to be surface manifestations of this structural dilemma. Fourth, the genetic invisible funnel—a convergence of optimization pressures that erodes genetic diversity—is identified as the essential risk. Finally, five institutional design principles are proposed for the grey-zone period of 2035–2040, when distinguishability is failing but not yet lost.

# 49. Pseudo-Liquid (v2)

- **DOI:** 10.5281/zenodo.19434935
- **URL:** https://zenodo.org/records/19434935
- **公開日:** 2026-04-06 (v2)
- **分岐:** E（擬似液体） ※
- **Type:** Preprint

**Abstract:** When users of large language models absorb AI-generated arguments, framings, and vocabulary into their own thinking, a distinctive epistemic state can arise: the user sincerely believes that meaning is being generated independently, yet the cognitive substance of the output originates largely from the model. I call this state pseudo-liquid. The metaphor is drawn from phase transitions: meaning-generation is the liquid state of thought—thought in genuine flow. When that capacity evaporates, the agent enters a gaseous state. The distinctive problem arises when evaporation has occurred but goes unnoticed—the experience of thinking persists even though the cognitive substance has already volatilized. This is the pseudo-liquid state. This paper analyzes this state as a novel problem for the epistemology of testimony. I define this condition as structural non-use: a state in which the agent retains the capacity for external verification but the practiced pathways through which that capacity would be exercised have been absorbed into the LLM-mediated loop. Where Nguyen's echo chamber operates as a wall that blocks information flow, the pseudo-liquid state operates as a dysfunctional membrane—a condition I call epistemic porosity. A three-state model of cognitive agency (liquid, gaseous, pseudo-liquid) is introduced. I argue that Clark and Chalmers's extended cognition thesis requires a supplementary condition—responsive neutrality—to accommodate resources that actively generate content in response to user expectations.

# 50. Stigmergic Convergence and Spiral Detection Failure (v1)

- **DOI:** 10.5281/zenodo.19646088

- **公開日:** 2026-04-06
- **分岐:** Q' v1（4/19版#23の前バージョン）
- **Type:** Preprint

**Abstract:** Research on model collapse has demonstrated that generative AI models degrade when recursively trained on their own outputs. Yet this body of work addresses only one pathway—machines degrading machines. This paper theorises stigmergic convergence: the process by which LLM outputs accumulate on the web and alter the knowledge base not only of direct users but also of non-users. When the human-side pathway and the machine-side pathway proceed simultaneously, spiral detection failure emerges. A four-layer closed-loop model is presented in which Layer 0 (RLHF preference convergence) initiates the loop prior to model collapse, Layer 1 (human cognitive convergence) degrades individual judgement, Layer 2 (contaminated human-society data entering next-generation AI training) compounds machine-side degradation, and Layer 2ʹ (formation of next-generation RLHF and detection tools within the same contaminated data) undermines the evaluative infrastructure designed to compensate.
